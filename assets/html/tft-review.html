Teamfight Tactics

Score: [8.6/10] Genre: [Auto Battler] Platform: [PC, Mobile] Release: [June 26, 2019]

Overview

Teamfight Tactics (TFT) is Riot Games' Auto Battler. This is a game I play regularly and enjoy improving on. I can put a lot of time into this game and the required strategies to become a better player. The game is free to play and require no microstransactions to play, everything is purely comesmetic.

Gameplay

The game is true to its genre, as the name entails, battles commence "automatically" and the majority of the player's job is to construct a team of characters are strong and synergize well together. TFT functions around economy and health as the player's main tools. You gain interest every 10 gold (up to 50) and gain extra gold based on your streaks, whether you win or lose. This plays back into health because you can lose streak to gain more gold at the cost of losing health, and as what is true in many other games, you lose when your health reaches 0. There are multiple rounds each stage, you take more damage from each loss as you progress through the game. There is a lot more nuance to the gameplay but the best way to learn is to watch pros play or play the game yourself. The characters, called units, range from 1-5 cost, and show up in your shop based on a percentage relating to your level. You spend money to both level up and roll your shop. Another important mechanic is that having 3 copies of the same unit increases its star level, which in turn increases it strength. There are also items you can craft, requiring 2 item components to create a full item, where a unit can hold up to 3.This can seem somewhat intimidating at first but as you play and watch other players, certain fundamental skills will be ingrained in you over time. You can find more information in the references section.

Story

TFT doesn't exactly have a story but has a simple premise that characters from the League of Legends universe are pulled in by entities called Little Legends or Tacticians to fight in these games known as the convergence. The story is definitely not the main focus for TFT and simply exists to provide somewhat of a "reason" for the characters from League of Legends to appear in TFT. Maybe in the future Riot Games will expand on this but for now, it is just an afterthought.

Visuals and Audio

Riot Games in all their games has done really well with how things look and sound, and TFT is no exception. You might think that even though they pull assets from League of Legends to use in TFT, the game would look relatively bland. However, this is not the case at all, the developers of TFT put a lot of effort into making all the units look unique to the theme of the set they are present in. They make the units feel like they are apart of the set and not just carbon copies. Aside from the actual game, Riot Games allows customization of your board (where you play) and your little legend/tactician (your character that you control). The only demerit is that these cosmetics are not free, but do not impact the game in any way. Better cosmetics cost more but a lot of them can be worth the price, excluding the skins that come from the gatcha mechanic Riot has implemented, those are dubious at best.

Performance

Similar to League of Legends, TFT uses the same engine so you can basically run it on a toaster, thats how little is required to play the game. So anyone can play the game if they want to, it is not limited to just PC, they have a mobile version as well. The game is very stable and if any issues arise, they are usually fixed very quickly and Riot will usually give a notice that they are aware of the issue and are working on a fix. I very rarely have any issues with the game regarding performance.

Final Verdict

All in all, TFT is a great game that has no barrier of entry and is a great way to pass time. The introduction can be somewhat intimidating but these types of games are where you learn by playing. There should be a lot of enjoyment to be had since the game has new sets every 3 months, allowing for new experiences to be had. One of the major critiques I can have is that the gatcha system for the cosmetics are somewhat predatory but at the very least they don't impact the game in any way. One I have to give credit for is that the devs for the game are really open and transparent about the game. One of the head developers, Mortdog, has weekly rundowns about the meta and patch rundowns when a new patch drops, making it easier to digest new changes and the devs throughts on why they made certain changes. In the end, I give TFT a 8.6/10.
